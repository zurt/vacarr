Research

Our ability to form and subsequently retrieve memories is essential for survival. Our memories are not veridical copies of our experiences, however, and my research aims to understand the factors that influence the content, quality, and durability of our memories across the lifespan. My research employs both behavioral and neuroimaging approaches, with the latter focusing on high-resolution imaging of the medial temporal lobe.

Bio

Born in Atlanta, GA, I attended the College of William & Mary as an undergraduate, majoring in Biological Psychology. Upon graduating, I worked at the Martinos Center for Biomedical Imaging at Massachusetts General Hospital as Chief Technician of the Center's MEG system. I then obtained a PhD in Neuroscience from UCLA , working with Barbara Knowlton and Steve Engel.

I subsequently obtained a postdoctoral position at Stanford University, where I was funded by an NIH postdoctoral fellowship and worked with Drs. Anthony Wagner and Geoffrey Kerchner. My postdoctoral research aimed to understand mechanisms of memory and how these mechanisms change with age – questions that I continue to investigate in my current position as a research associate at Stanford.
